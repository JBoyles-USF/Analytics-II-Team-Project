\section{Introduction}

This project aims to utilize historical ball bearing manufacturing data to predict 
defectiveness of manufactured parts and to understand which combinations of control 
settings typically lead to defective production. The goal is to create an interpretable 
model that accurately predicts whether a ball bearing will be classified as defective 
after several destructive and non-destructive tests, based on a set of predictor variables.

The dataset contains approximately 230,000 samples with 30 predictor variables. Each sample 
is labeled as defective (class 1) or non-defective (class 0), which is the target variable 
we aim to predict. By understanding the factors that contribute to defects, corrective 
action can be taken on controllable system inputs to reduce future defects. Reduced defect 
rates directly lower scrap, rework, resource, and labor costs in the manufacturing process.

However, this problem presents several challenges: the dataset is large and high-dimensional, 
the classes may be imbalanced (with relatively few defective samples), and manufacturing 
processes can involve complex, nonlinear interactions between variables. Additionally, 
interpretability is critical, as predictions must provide actionable insights for engineers 
and management.

In this project, we aim to:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Develop an interpretable predictive model for classifying defective ball bearings.
    \item Identify and analyze key process variables that most strongly influence defect rates.
    \item Explore how the model’s insights can guide corrective actions to reduce defects and improve manufacturing efficiency.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these aims outline a plan to leverage historical manufacturing data for 
actionable defect prediction and process improvement.

\section*{Model and Method}

\subsection*{Class Imbalance - Preferred Metrics}
The dataset comprises 229,788 ball-bearing test records featuring 30 numerical predictors and a binary response variable. With only 458 defective samples, standard classifiers tend to favor the majority class, yielding high accuracy that can be misleading. In manufacturing, false negatives are more costly than false positives, as they can lead to diminished customer confidence, expensive product recalls, or liability lawsuits. 

Consequently, accuracy is not an appropriate metric. Instead, recall, precision, F1 Score, and the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (PR-AUC) are the primary metrics. Because the defective class represents only 0.2\% of the dataset, using ROC AUC can overly estimate performance. A study proved ROC AUC is not dependable if the true positive sample is less than 3\% of the total (Imani et al., 2024). PR-AUC is preferred because it reflects performance exclusively on the defective class.

Using a regular F1 score can allow the majority class to dominate the result:
$$F1_{regular} = 2 \cdot \frac{P_{overall} \cdot R_{overall}}{P_{overall} + R_{overall}}$$
$F1_{minority}$ avoids this masking effect by computing metrics only for the defective class:
$$F1_{minority} = 2 \cdot \frac{P_{minority} \cdot R_{minority}}{P_{minority} + R_{minority}}$$

\subsection*{Class Imbalance - Modification on Training Data}
To address the imbalance, four strategies are employed:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Random Oversampling:} Duplicates minority samples to improve recall, but risks overfitting.
    \item \textbf{Random Undersampling:} Removes majority samples to balance classes but may lead to information loss.
    \item \textbf{SMOTE:} Generates synthetic defective samples by interpolation to refine decision boundaries.
    \item \textbf{Class-Weighted training:} Adjusts the learning objective to penalize minority-class errors more heavily.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Model Considerations}
We evaluate four primary models: Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, and AdaBoost/XGBoost.

\subsubsection*{Logistic Regression}
Logistic Regression models the probability that a bearing is defective:
$$P(defective|x) = \sigma(\beta_{0} + \beta^{\top}x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\beta_{0} + \beta^{\top}x)}}$$
Each coefficient $\beta_{j}$ represents the change in the log-odds of a defect associated with a one-unit increase in reading $j$.

\subsubsection*{Decision Trees}
Decision Trees provide transparency through logical binary splits. Purity is measured using Gini Impurity:
$$Gini(S) = \sum_{k \in \{0,1\}} p_{k}(1 - p_{k})$$
A split into child nodes $S_{L}$ and $S_{R}$ is evaluated by the reduction in impurity:
$$\Delta = Gini(S) - \left(\frac{|S_{L}|}{|S|}Gini(S_{L}) + \frac{|S_{R}|}{|S|}Gini(S_{R})\right)$$

\subsubsection*{Random Forest}
Random Forest aggregates many trees trained on bootstrap samples. The final prediction is obtained by majority vote:
$$\hat{y} = mode\{\hat{y}^{(1)}, \hat{y}^{(2)}, \dots, \hat{y}^{(N)}\}$$
To address imbalance, the \textit{Balanced Random Forest} variant is used, where each tree is trained on a balanced bootstrap sample to improve recall.

\subsubsection*{Boosting}
AdaBoost builds an ensemble of weak learners by reweighing training samples. Misclassified samples have their weights $w_{j}^{i}$ increased. The classifier's importance weight is:
$$\alpha_{t} = \frac{1}{2} \ln\left(\frac{1 - \epsilon_{t}}{\epsilon_{t}}\right)$$
Sample weights are updated as follows:
$$w_{j}^{i+1} = \frac{w_{j}^{l}}{Z_{i}} \times \begin{cases} e^{-\alpha_{i}}, & C_{i}(x_{j}) = y_{j} \\ e^{\alpha_{i}}, & C_{i}(x_{j}) \neq y_{j} \end{cases}$$
